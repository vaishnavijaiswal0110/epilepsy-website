3. Causes of Epilepsy

Epilepsy has many causes that disrupt the brain's normal electrical activity. These causes fall into several broad categories: genetic, structural, infectious, metabolic, immune, and unknown.

Genetic causes involve inherited or random mutations in genes that manage ion channels, neurotransmitters, or brain development. These mutations can make neurons overly excitable and more prone to seizures.

Structural causes refer to physical damage or abnormalities in the brain such as head injuries, brain tumors, cysts, surgical scars, or developmental abnormalities before birth.

Problems during birth and early childhood, including lack of oxygen, premature birth, low birth weight, infections, or brain hemorrhage, can lead to epilepsy later in life.

Infectious causes include diseases such as meningitis, encephalitis, brain abscesses, malaria, and parasitic infections like neurocysticercosis.

Vascular causes such as strokes, brain hemorrhages, or abnormal blood vessels can disrupt blood flow and damage brain tissue.

Metabolic and systemic disorders like low blood sugar, electrolyte imbalance, liver or kidney failure, or inherited metabolic conditions can trigger seizures.

Immune-related causes, such as autoimmune encephalitis, occur when the immune system attacks brain tissue, causing inflammation and seizures.

In many cases, doctors cannot identify the exact cause. These cases are classified as epilepsy of unknown cause.
