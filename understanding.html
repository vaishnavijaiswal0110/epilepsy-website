Understanding Epilepsy

Epilepsy is a complex, long-term neurological condition where a person frequently experiences repetitive, unprovoked seizures due to abnormal and excessive electrical activity in the brain.

Normally, neurons communicate through regulated electrical and chemical signals. This communication allows the brain to control thoughts, movement, sensation, and behavior. In epilepsy, this balance is disrupted. Groups of neurons fire suddenly, together, and uncontrollably.

These abnormal signals can start in one specific area of the brain (focal seizures) or affect both hemispheres from the beginning (generalized seizures). The symptoms depend on which brain regions are involved. They can range from brief lapses in awareness, staring spells, unusual sensations, muscle stiffness, jerking movements, loss of consciousness, or convulsions.

Epilepsy can result from genetic factors, complications during brain development, head injuries, strokes, brain infections, tumors, or scarring of brain tissue. In many cases, no clear cause is found.

Despite its long-term nature, epilepsy is not contagious and does not automatically affect intelligence. With proper diagnosis, treatment, and support, it is manageable. Many people with epilepsy can control their seizures and live independent, productive lives.
